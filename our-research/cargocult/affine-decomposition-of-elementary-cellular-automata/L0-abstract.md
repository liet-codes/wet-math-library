Every elementary cellular automaton (ECA) rule can be uniquely represented by an affine decomposition over F₂, which separates a linear base from a nonlinear perturbation, thereby structurally explaining Wolfram’s behavioral classes. This decomposition creates a two-axis classification revealing that essentially all complex and chaotic behaviors arise from perturbations of specific fractal affine bases. Notably, the only proven universal ECA, Rule 110, occupies a unique algebraic position characterized by an asymmetric fractal base, a cubic term in its perturbation, and a non-saturated, mixed-degree nonlinear signature. Comparisons demonstrate that the specific identity and symmetry of perturbation monomials significantly dictate a rule's qualitative dynamics, with maximal algebraic complexity often constraining rather than fostering complex behavior.
