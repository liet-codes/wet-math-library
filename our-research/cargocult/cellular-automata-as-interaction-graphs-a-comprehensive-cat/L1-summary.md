# L1 Structural Overview

## Title
Cellular Automata as Interaction Graphs: A Comprehensive Catalog of Types, Shared Rule Spaces, and Tractability Boundaries

## Author
Brooklyn Rose and her robots (Cargo Cult Research)

## Main Thesis
This paper establishes a unified framework for Cellular Automata (CA) by interpreting all standard CA types as "interaction graphs"—periodic, layered, directed graphs with uniform in-degree. Through systematic cataloging, analysis of shared rule spaces, and precise determination of computational tractability, the authors reveal that conventionally named CA types represent a small, highly symmetric subset of a much larger, largely unexplored landscape of computational substrates.

## Section Structure and Key Claims

The paper begins with an **Abstract** outlining its core contributions, leading into a comprehensive **1. Introduction**. This section grounds the work in the generalized Cayley graph framework (Arrighi, Martiel, Nesme, 2012, 2018), specializing it to "interaction graphs" to enable computationally tractable, exhaustive analysis. A CA's "type" is defined as the isomorphism class of its interaction graph, with rule space determined by alphabet size `k` and in-degree `n`, and its symmetry group by graph automorphisms and alphabet permutations. Key contributions include: (1) a systematic catalog of standard CA types; (2) enumeration of one-dimensional interaction graph types, revealing a vast number of unnamed graphs; (3) an algebraic characterization of block CA embeddings; and (4) precise tractability boundaries for exhaustive rule analysis. The authors differentiate their approach from prior work (e.g., Arrighi and Dowek, 2013; Hedlund, 1969) by prioritizing *algebraic and computational* characterization over topological or dynamical aspects. Their contribution regarding block CA embedding (building on Toffoli and Margolus, 1987) is noted as a *complete algebraic characterization* of the embedding's image.

**2. The Interaction Graph Framework** formally defines an interaction graph as a directed graph with layered vertices, uniform in-degree `n`, and periodic structure. A cellular automaton is then a pair `(G, f)` where `G` is an interaction graph and `f: Σⁿ→Σ` is the local rule. CA isomorphism is defined via layer-preserving graph isomorphisms and alphabet permutations, yielding the symmetry group `Aut(G) × S_k`. The **Rule Space and Its Algebraic Structure** specifies the size `k^(kⁿ)` and notes that each rule has a graph-independent Algebraic Normal Form (ANF) over `𝔽ₖ` (for prime `k`). **Nested Embedding** explains how `n`-input rules can be extended to `(n+1)`-input rules. **Totalistic and Outer-Totalistic Subspaces** are described as constrained rule subsets within an interaction graph, invariant under input permutations, with their sizes cataloged and the Game of Life identified as an example.

**3. Catalog of CA Types as Interaction Graphs** systematically enumerates interaction graph types. **One-Dimensional Types: Offset Sets** defines 1D graphs by their input offset sets, distinguishing standard contiguous types from a rapidly growing number of unnamed non-contiguous types. The **Complete Catalog by Input Count** details shared rule spaces by `k` and `n`:
*   `n=3, k=2` (ECA): 256 rules, 88 equivalence classes.
*   `n=3, k=3` (3-color ECA): ~7.63x10¹² rules, ~635 billion classes.
*   `n=4, k=2` (Central Shared Space): 65,536 rules, including 5 named types (1D contiguous, 2D Triangular, Ladder graph, 2nd-order ECA, 1D Block) and 21 unnamed 1D types, plus 2D Archimedean tilings. This `n=4` space is highlighted as ideal for cross-type comparison.
*   `n=5, k=2` (Billion-Rule Space): ~4.3x10⁹ rules, with over 40 distinct types (e.g., 1D radius-2, 2D Von Neumann, 2D Kagome).
Higher `n` spaces (e.g., `n=7` with 3D Von Neumann, `n=9` with 2D Moore) are also presented. The **Growth of the Type Count** section underscores that named types constitute a tiny fraction of the total. **Block, Second-Order, and Higher-Dimensional Types** are characterized as specific interaction graphs, including a comprehensive list of all 11 Archimedean tilings as 2D types. The **Scope and Limitations of the Catalog** clarifies that non-Euclidean or non-abelian Cayley graphs are excluded due to lack of periodicity, which is crucial for *exhaustive* analysis within this framework.

**4. Block CA Embedding: A Worked Example** shows that 256 1D binary Margolus block CA rules map to only 149 *unique* effective 4-input rules, covering 0.23% of the `n=4` rule space. A key finding is that while trivially-embedded ECAs and block-reachable rules overlap in 16 *affine* rules, their *nonlinear* structures are entirely disjoint. This demonstrates that interaction graph topology imposes **Monomial Accessibility** constraints, meaning different CA types access fundamentally distinct algebraic subsets of the shared rule space.

**5. Tractability Boundaries** precisely delineates exhaustive classification limits: all binary CAs up to `n=5` (~1.07 billion equivalence classes) are feasible on a laptop, and all 3-color elementary CAs (~635 billion classes) are within reach using cloud GPU resources. Larger spaces necessitate **Dense Sampling** or are **Beyond Current Tractability** for exhaustive methods.

**6. Discussion** synthesizes the results. The **Interaction Graph as Type** argument posits that traditional CA types are merely isomorphism classes of interaction graphs. The identified **Unexplored Territory** (many unnamed non-contiguous 1D types) suggests an experimental program: comparing the dynamics of *identical rules* across different interaction graphs to separate the effects of algebraic structure from causal topology. The paper emphasizes its complementary relationship with **Causal Graph Dynamics** (Arrighi and Dowek, 2013), focusing on fixed topology for exhaustive rule analysis. The authors conclude that **Algebraic Structure and Causal Topology** are distinct but interacting components, with topology even constraining the accessible algebraic landscape.

**7. Conclusion** reiterates the paper's main achievements: a unified framework, the discovery of a vast unexplored CA landscape, the integration of Archimedean tilings, the finding of algebraically disjoint nonlinear subspaces, and a clear roadmap for exhaustive classification.

## Connections to Other Group Work

The paper's core is the rigorous definition and cataloging of "interaction graphs," which are specialized forms of "generalized Cayley graphs" (Arrighi, Martiel, Nesme, 2012, 2018) and distinct from "causal graph dynamics" (Arrighi and Dowek, 2013). This systematic, combinatorial, and exhaustive approach to classifying computational "substrates" and their associated rule spaces directly aligns with a "wet-math" philosophy, particularly in its emphasis on an "experimental program" for disentangling algebraic and topological influences on dynamics. The use of "isomorphism classes" for CA types touches upon concepts from category theory, though not explicitly developed. No connections to Grothendieck anabelian geometry, Shannon clusters, or proportional math are made.
