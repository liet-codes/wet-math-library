Before Prediction
The Mathematics Was Right. The Ontology Was Optional.
Working draft — March 2026

In 2023, researchers at the University Medical Center Hamburg-Eppendorf published a study of spike-timing-dependent plasticity that should unsettle anyone who takes the computational metaphor for the brain at face value. Using optogenetic stimulation — light rather than electrodes, which meant they could revisit the same synapses days later — they measured how hippocampal synapses change in response to precisely timed neural firing. At twenty minutes, the textbook story held: synapses strengthened when the presynaptic neuron fired before the postsynaptic neuron and weakened in the reverse order. This is the result that has been interpreted for decades as the brain learning causal structure — the synapse discovering that A predicts B, one connection at a time.
At three days — the timescale of actual memory — the causal asymmetry was gone. Both orderings produced stable potentiation. The only condition that failed was asynchronous firing: neurons that didn't fire together at all. The title: "Spike-timing-dependent plasticity rewards synchrony rather than causality."
If the foundational unit of neural modification stabilizes co-firing rather than causal order at the timescale that matters, then the brain's long-term architecture is organized around synchrony. The causal reading of STDP — the interpretation that has underwritten two decades of computational models of learning — describes a transient phenomenon. What persists is more physical and less computational: neurons that fire together wire together, regardless of which fires first.
This result arrives in the context of a broader convergence. Over the past five years, findings from condensed matter physics, motor neuroscience, comparative neurobiology, machine learning, and the philosophy of computation have been pointing in the same direction — not at a flaw in the dominant framework of cognitive neuroscience, but at a subtle mismatch between its mathematics and its interpretation of that mathematics.
The framework in question is predictive processing, and it deserves its prominence. Karl Friston's variational free energy principle is a genuine mathematical achievement: any persistent system with a boundary separating internal from external states will, as a consequence of its persistence, develop internal dynamics that parameterize the statistics of its external coupling. The system's dynamics will decrease the divergence between this parameterization and the actual external statistics. This holds for slime molds, thermostats, rivers, and brains. It tells us something deep about the relationship between persistence, coupling, and statistical structure. The experimental program built on this mathematics — in motor control, perception, learning, and attention — has been the most productive in cognitive neuroscience for fifteen years.
The framework takes this general mathematics and adds an interpretive layer: the internal parameterization is a "generative model," the divergence is "prediction error," the dynamics that decrease it are "inference." The system is now described as computing. In the cerebellar-motor domain, where climbing fiber error signals have been measured and circuitry identified, this interpretation earns its keep. But the interpretation is not the mathematics. The mathematics says: persistent coupled systems renegotiate their coupling dynamics in a direction that decreases divergence. The interpretation adds: this renegotiation is computation.
In 2026, David Wolpert and Jan Korbel published a paper in the Journal of Physics: Complexity that makes the distinction precise. For naturally occurring dynamical systems, the identification of a computation within those dynamics is a mapping we impose — not a fact we discover. A system whose dynamics satisfy the formal constraints of Bayesian inference is not necessarily "doing inference." The description is correct. The mechanism is different.
The framework's error was treating the interpretation as coextensive with the mathematics — assuming that because the free energy math applies universally, the computational interpretation applies universally too. But universally valid mathematics and universally applicable ontology are different things.
What the Mathematics Is Tracking
If the computational interpretation is a conceptual layer on top of the mathematics, what is the mathematics actually describing? Three results, spanning the synapse, the network, and the system, converge on an answer.
The Anisimova result provides the synaptic foundation: at biologically relevant timescales, plasticity rewards synchrony. A 2022 paper by Klaus Gansel proposed the network consequence. Neurons repeatedly excited by a specific input sequence learn, through this synchrony-rewarding plasticity, to respond to the onset of the sequence rather than to each element. Repeating patterns are temporally compressed until some or all signals are synchronized. The mechanism is topology-independent — spatial position doesn't matter, only temporal structure — and the resulting synchronous ensembles are modulated by ongoing network oscillations providing, in Gansel's phrase, "a dynamic, flexible context for the synaptic integration of distributed signals."
This connects to the leading edge of systems neuroscience. Work by Krishnan Shenoy and Mark Churchland has shown that motor cortex activity lies on low-dimensional manifolds — smooth surfaces in the high-dimensional space of possible activity patterns. Motor commands are trajectories along these manifolds, not discrete instructions. The Mosers' grid cells are translation operators on a neural manifold. Head-direction networks are ring attractors. In each case, the neural system preserves relational structure through dynamical operations on a manifold, not through symbolic computation over representations. Gansel's temporal compression is a candidate for how these manifold structures form: through plasticity-driven compression of recurring relational patterns into the network's dynamical geometry.
The third result is systems-level. In 2025, Di Ventra and colleagues showed that long-range order — correlated activity spanning a network, with power-law avalanche statistics — can be sustained by timescale separation between fast neural dynamics and slow resource dynamics. The order is robust across a wide parameter range. When timescale separation narrows, the order degrades. When it collapses, the order is destroyed. This reframes the twenty-year criticality debate: the power-law statistics are real, but the mechanism is timescale separation, not proximity to a phase transition.
Criticality, however, is not thereby made irrelevant. Metastable regimes can be intransitive and spontaneously bifurcate. Biological systems can be closer to or farther from the dynamics that criticality mathematics describes, and transitions between configurations — territorial reorganization, behavioral mode-switching, the loser effect in social contests — genuinely exhibit critical-like scaling. What the broader dynamical systems literature reveals is a landscape of regimes: robust ordered phases sustained by timescale separation; fast-slow bifurcations where slow variables drive abrupt reorganization; metastable itinerant dynamics where systems move among quasi-stable configurations; Griffiths phases where heterogeneous networks produce quasi-critical behavior over extended parameter regions; and genuine critical points. Biological systems navigate this landscape. Criticality is real mathematics describing real features of the terrain. It is not the terrain itself.
A 2021 paper in Brain Research provided the formal connection between this physical substrate and the mathematics of inference. The authors showed that an entirely unsupervised, memoryless reservoir computing network exhibits every hallmark of predictive processing — anticipatory behavior, error-like correction, apparent updating — through homeostatic dynamics alone. Entrained dynamical systems satisfy the formal constraints of approximate Bayesian inference without implementing inference. The Bayesian description applies. The mechanism is not Bayesian. This is exactly what Friston's own ergodicity result predicts: any persistent coupled system will, over time, appear to minimize free energy. The F-minimization is a consequence of persistence, not of computation.
Together: synchrony-rewarding plasticity at the synapse produces temporally compressed ensembles organized on low-dimensional manifolds, sustained by timescale separation within a rich dynamical landscape. This is the physical process the free energy mathematics describes. Not prediction. Not inference. The formation, maintenance, and renegotiation of coherent coupling between a dynamical system and whatever it is coupled to. Memory, in this picture, is not stored models retrieved for prediction. It is curvature in the coupling substrate — modified weights, compressed patterns, shifted thresholds — that biases future dynamics without representing anything.
Coupling Renegotiation Across Scales
The framework organizes evidence that currently sits in disciplinary silos. The question across all of it is the same: what does the general process of coupling renegotiation look like at different scales?
At the chemical scale, Physarum polycephalum solves mazes and approximates optimal transport networks through calcium-wave oscillations — no neurons, no models. Its "memory" is residual oscillatory bias: curvature in the coupling substrate. Ant colonies optimize foraging through the coupling topography of pheromone landscapes. At the trophic scale, five predator species work a sardine bait ball in coordinated relay, never communicating, never attacking each other — coordination emerging from their individual coupling with the same prey resource. At the social scale, fish schools coordinate through lateral-line phase-locking; starling murmurations exhibit flock-wide correlations that the Toner-Tu theory shows can be sustained by nonequilibrium alignment interactions without proximity to a critical point. In none of these cases does the computational vocabulary add explanatory power that the dynamical description lacks.
The wolf pack is where something qualitatively shifts. A pack maintains role differentiation across hours of pursuit, and remembers territorial structure across years. The coupling between organism and niche is now rich enough — enough timescales, enough internal degrees of freedom — that the distinction between "map" and "territory" becomes a meaningful phenomenon. Not because the wolf builds a model, but because the coupling carries the territory's structure as curvature: trails worn, marking patterns deposited, behavioral thresholds shaped by experience. And the coupling is co-dynamical — the wolf changes the territory through marking and prey depletion. The map shapes the territory and the territory shapes the map. This is what Donald Hoffman's interface theory — in its softer, non-eliminativist reading — describes: coupling shaped by fitness relevance rather than veridical representation. The organism is coupled to a salience landscape, not to raw physics.
Corvids provide the cleanest test case. With no layered cortex — a nuclear-organized pallium independently evolved, running on associative pallio-striatal loops — crows achieve tool use, food caching, social cognition, mirror self-recognition, and play. Their territorial dynamics are instructive: extended family groups maintain fluid boundaries through vocal display and spatial signaling. The loser effect — an animal that loses a contest becoming more likely to lose subsequently regardless of opponent quality — is consistent with the coupling topography having been renegotiated through a reorganization event, with the new topography biasing future interactions. This makes a testable prediction that differs from the computational account: the effect should be modifiable by changing the coupling context (the territorial environment), not just by updating information about the rival. Play behavior — young crows sliding down snowy rooftops, wolf pups wrestling — is the organism exploring available renegotiation pathways under conditions of safety: testing what coupling configurations are accessible.
Two empirical stories are particularly revealing because they directly distinguish the coupling framework from the computational one.
Anesthesia
If coherent coupling is sustained by timescale separation, pharmacologically disrupting the timescale bridge should degrade coherence while leaving local dynamics intact. A 2024 multi-anesthetic study found exactly this: five chemically distinct anesthetics all preferentially disrupted cortical-subcortical coupling — the timescale bridge — while local cortical networks remained relatively intact but became stereotypic, locked into a narrow repertoire of states. Perturbational complexity confirmed the picture: wakefulness supports long-range, temporally differentiated cortical responses to stimulation; propofol and sevoflurane reduce these to brief, local, stereotypic reactions.
Ketamine provides the critical dissociation. Acting through NMDA blockade rather than GABA enhancement, it produces behavioral unresponsiveness while preserving complex cortical dynamics and high perturbational complexity — and is associated with vivid internal experience. External coupling is disrupted. Internal timescale architecture is maintained. The computational ontology, which treats consciousness as accurate prediction of sensory input, would predict that blocking sensory coupling produces experiential silence. The coupling framework predicts that internal coherence can persist independently of external coupling. Ketamine confirms the coupling prediction.
A 2025 study in Nature Human Behaviour added a striking dimension: under anesthesia, individual brains become less distinguishable from each other. The loss follows the sensory-association axis — the most experience-shaped regions lose their individual character first. The curvature that makes each brain unique is carried in the long-range-ordered phase sustained by timescale separation. Disrupt the bridge, the curvature collapses toward generic. When the anesthetic wears off, the patterns return. The curvature was not destroyed — only rendered dynamically inaccessible.
The Mastery Transition
Longitudinal piano training studies map a transition that the coupling framework predicts and the computational framework does not. Novice pianists show high cortical activation — dorsal premotor and parietal cortices engaged in spatial attention and deliberate error correction. Within six months, this engagement vanishes entirely. No single model of brain plasticity explains the time course. The skill transitions from cortical mediation to subcortical entrainment. The instrument is absorbed into the coupling.
The same transition appears at the social scale. A 2024 meta-analysis of inter-brain synchrony in teamwork found that in expert high-tempo tasks, improved coordination coincides with reduced inter-brain coupling — "team neural efficiency." The computational ontology predicts that better coordination requires better mutual modeling. The coupling framework predicts the opposite: once shared coupling is well-established, it is self-sustaining. Active renegotiation is needed during formation. At mastery, the coupling maintains itself.
Long-term meditators self-induce high-amplitude gamma synchrony that correlates with years of practice, suggesting coupling topography is not only shaped by experience but deliberately cultivable. Inter-brain synchrony appears across every domain where coordination has been measured: teams, musicians, conversational partners, classrooms, even online gaming without physical co-presence. And a 2025 study found that inter-brain synchrony between conversational strangers correlates with emergent shared social identity.
The developmental arc is consistent. Novice: high active coupling, computational description fits. Expert: self-sustaining coupling, reduced active renegotiation, coupling description fits. The free energy mathematics describes both regimes. The computational interpretation captures one.
Carl Rogers, working in the 1950s, observed a clinical version of the landscape this framework describes. Clients in therapy exhibited "violent fluctuation in the concept of the self" — a minor incident could flip the entire self-image, like the old hag / young woman gestalt illusion. Rogers noted this was not gradual learning: "The product was clearly a gestalt, a configuration in which the alteration of one minor aspect could completely alter the whole pattern." What he observed, without the vocabulary, was metastable coupling dynamics: a coherent configuration that can undergo rapid reorganization when a perturbation interacts with the configuration's intransitive geometry.
What Computation Actually Is
One of the reasons the computational interpretation has been so persistent is that "computation" is used loosely. In common usage, any system that processes information is "computing." Under this usage, the claim that the brain computes is trivially true and scientifically empty.
The formal philosophy of computation is more precise. In Gualtiero Piccinini's mechanistic account — the most rigorous in the current literature — a computing system is a mechanism that manipulates medium-independent vehicles under an organized functional scheme. This means: the same computation can in principle be realized in different physical media; there are norm-governed transitions that distinguish correct from incorrect state changes; and there is a functional organization that makes specific transitions count as the implementation of a specific computation.
These are demanding criteria. They are what digital computers satisfy. And they are satisfied through engineering — through what the MIT Computation Structures literature calls the "digital abstraction": physical systems disciplined into discrete symbolic states by assigning voltage ranges to 0 and 1, building in noise margins, enforcing clocking, and stabilizing state transitions. A digital computer is not naturally discrete. It is a physical system whose richer dynamics have been suppressed to approximate the boundary conditions of formal computation. The formal computation is the idealized limit. The real machine approaches it.
The brain's dynamics do not satisfy Piccinini's criteria except locally and approximately. Neural signals are continuous and noisy. State transitions are not norm-governed in the formal sense — there is no system-level distinction between "correct" and "incorrect" firing patterns. The computation is not medium-independent — the specific biophysical properties of the tissue (ion channel kinetics, dendritic morphology, metabolic constraints) shape the dynamics in ways that are not captured by any medium-independent description. Even in the cerebellum — the strongest case — what is happening is continuous analog transformation of signals through a dynamical circuit shaped by plasticity. You can describe this as computation and the description is useful. But the mechanism doesn't satisfy the criteria that the philosophy of computation identifies as constitutive of formal computation.
This is not a deficiency of the brain. It may be a deficiency of the concept. Formal computation, as Piccinini defines it, is an idealized regime at one extreme of a continuum. The continuum runs from coupled dynamics (the primitive level — organism and environment in continuous mutual influence) through task-relative modeling (where the system's dynamics are compressed into variables relevant for action) through representation (where some compressed variables stably preserve task-relevant relations across contexts) to formal computation (where representations become discretized and norm-governed enough to satisfy the mathematical definition). Real biological cognition operates along this continuum. Digital computers operate at its extreme. The brain operates mostly in the rich middle, where the dynamics are too continuous, too noisy, too context-sensitive, and too coupled to body and environment to count as formal computation, but where they support flexible, adaptive, relationship-preserving behavior that formal computation cannot replicate.
The full development of this continuum — and its implications for understanding intelligence as something broader than computation — exceeds the scope of this piece. But the continuum matters here because it shows where the computational interpretation of the free energy mathematics sits. It sits at the formal end of the continuum: the regime where coupling dynamics are regular enough, directional enough, and discretizable enough to be captured by a computable function. The free energy mathematics applies along the entire continuum. The computational interpretation applies at one end. The framework's error was treating one end as the whole.
The Expansion
The predictive processing framework gave us mathematics that describes how persistent coupled systems renegotiate their dynamics. That contribution is genuine and lasting. The mathematics applies to every persistent coupled system, from slime molds to ecosystems, far more broadly than its architects may have intended.
The computational ontology layered on top — models, predictions, errors, inference — is a conceptual lens. It is what happens when the concepts of computational theory are imposed on general mathematics. The lens earns its keep in specific regimes: fast, regular, directional coupling dynamics with a dedicated anticipatory channel, where the dynamics approach the boundary conditions of formal computation. Outside those regimes — which is most of what the brain does and most of what biological intelligence is — the mathematics still applies but the computational concepts are descriptive apparatus rather than mechanistic identification.
The physical process the mathematics describes is coupling formation through synchrony-rewarding plasticity, temporal compression into manifold structure, and timescale-separated maintenance within a landscape of dynamical regimes. Criticality is real mathematics describing real transitions in this landscape. Memory is curvature in the coupling substrate. Mastery is the transition from active renegotiation to self-sustaining coupling. And the organism-environment relationship is co-dynamical: the map shapes the territory and the territory shapes the map, with the coupling topography sculpted by evolution toward fitness-relevant salience rather than veridical representation.
The evidence for this picture is synaptic (STDP rewards synchrony at biologically relevant timescales), pharmacological (anesthesia disrupts timescale separation, ketamine dissociates external from internal coupling), ethological (from slime molds to corvid territorial dynamics), developmental (expert performance shows reduced active coupling, not better prediction), social (inter-brain synchrony tracks coordination quality and decreases with mastery), computational (entrained dynamics satisfy Bayesian constraints without implementing inference), and clinical (the self-concept reorganizes as a metastable gestalt, not as a gradual model update).
The missing pieces are specific: the formal derivation establishing the mathematical relationship between coupling dynamics with timescale separation and variational free energy minimization; an independent formalism for coupling renegotiation; controlled experiments that distinguish coupling-renegotiation predictions from computational predictions on the same phenomenon; and the full development of the continuum from coupled dynamics through representation to formal computation — the question of what intelligence is, if not fundamentally computation.
That last question is the one this piece opens without answering. The answer will require understanding how biological systems navigate the rich middle of the continuum — how they support flexible, context-sensitive, relationship-preserving behavior without satisfying the boundary conditions of formal computation. That is a harder question than "is the brain a prediction engine?" It is also, the evidence increasingly suggests, the right one.
